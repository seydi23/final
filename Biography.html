Muhammad Ali, born Cassius Marcellus Clay Jr. on January 17, 1942, in Louisville, Kentucky, USA, was one of the most iconic and influential figures of the 20th century. His life story is one of triumph, resilience, and activism. Here's a comprehensive biography of "The Greatest"

Early Life and Boxing Beginnings

Childhood in Louisville

Ali grew up in a predominantly African-American neighborhood in Louisville. He faced racial segregation and discrimination from an early age.

Introduction to Boxing

His journey in boxing began at the age of 12 when a police officer, Joe Martin, introduced him to the sport as a way to channel his energy positively.

Amateur Boxing Career

Olympic Gold Medal

Ali won the gold medal in the light heavyweight division at the 1960 Rome Olympics at the age of 18, representing the United States. This marked the beginning of his illustrious boxing career.

Professional Boxing Career

1964: Rise to Prominence

Ali, then known as Cassius Clay, stunned the world by defeating Sonny Liston to become the world heavyweight champion at the age of 22.

Conversion to Islam and Name Change

After joining the Nation of Islam, Ali changed his name from Cassius Clay to Muhammad Ali, signifying his spiritual and cultural identity.

Ali-Frazier Trilogy

Ali engaged in three legendary bouts with Joe Frazier, including the iconic "Fight of the Century" in 1971 and the brutal "Thrilla in Manila" in 1975.

Rivalry with George Foreman

In 1974, Ali employed his famous "rope-a-dope" strategy to defeat George Foreman in the "Rumble in the Jungle," reclaiming the heavyweight championship.

Three-Time Heavyweight Champion

Ali became the first boxer to win the heavyweight championship three times, solidifying his status as one of the greatest of all time.

Legacy of Activism

Ali was known for his outspokenness on social and political issues, including civil rights, racial equality, and opposition to the Vietnam War. His refusal to be drafted into the military led to his suspension from boxing and became a symbol of resistance.

Later Years and Legacy

Retirement

Ali retired from boxing in 1981 with a record of 56 wins and 5 losses.

Battle with Parkinson's Disease

In his later years, Ali battled Parkinson's disease, a condition likely exacerbated by his years in the ring.

Global Icon and Humanitarian

Ali's legacy extends beyond boxing; he is remembered as a global icon of peace, tolerance, and humanitarianism. His charisma, wit, and commitment to his principles continue to inspire millions worldwide.

Death and Posthumous Honors

Ali passed away on June 3, 2016, at the age of 74. His death prompted an outpouring of tributes from around the world, and he is remembered as a sports legend and cultural icon.
